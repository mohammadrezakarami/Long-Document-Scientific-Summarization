Abstract:
Scientific papers are difficult to summarize because they are long, section-structured, and densely packed with technical claims, numerical results, and domain-specific terminology. We present a section-aware summarization system that combines hierarchical inference with claim-level evidence retrieval to improve the faithfulness of summaries for long scientific documents. Instead of summarizing the full paper in a single pass, the proposed system first summarizes each major section, then synthesizes a final structured summary conditioned on the section summaries. To assess factual support, each generated claim is matched to the most relevant source passages using dense retrieval over section-aware chunks. Experiments on a subset of arXiv papers show that the method improves ROUGE-L by 2.4 points over a strong single-pass baseline and reduces unsupported claims by 18 percent in manual review. The approach is especially effective on papers longer than 6,000 words, where naive truncation often removes critical result and limitation details.

Introduction:
Long-document scientific summarization remains challenging even for modern instruction-tuned language models. While recent large language models can generate fluent summaries, they often miss key contributions, confuse methods with results, or hallucinate unsupported conclusions when the source paper exceeds practical context limits. This issue is especially visible in research papers where important information is distributed across introduction, method, experiments, discussion, and limitations sections. A model that sees only the beginning of a paper may produce a plausible summary while omitting decisive evidence from later sections.

In this work, we focus on faithful summarization rather than purely fluent generation. Our main objective is to build a system that preserves problem formulation, technical method, experimental findings, and limitations in a structured output. We argue that two design choices are critical. First, the summarizer should be section-aware so that it can treat method, results, and conclusion differently. Second, the system should provide an evidence support signal for generated claims so that users can inspect which statements are strongly supported by the paper and which are only weakly grounded.

Method:
The proposed framework has four stages. In the first stage, we parse the paper into section-aware chunks, prioritizing abstract, introduction, method, experiments, results, discussion, and conclusion. In the second stage, each section is summarized independently by an instruction-tuned language model. Section-level prompts emphasize different goals depending on the source section. For example, method summaries emphasize architecture, training procedure, and assumptions, while results summaries emphasize numerical outcomes, comparisons, and statistical significance when available.

In the third stage, the model receives all section summaries and generates a final structured summary with the fields TL;DR, Problem, Method, Key Contributions, Results, and Limitations. This synthesis step explicitly instructs the model not to invent numerical improvements and to write "Not specified" if a detail is missing from the source. In the fourth stage, we split the final summary into sentence-level claims and retrieve top evidence passages from the source paper using dense embeddings over section-aware chunks. Each claim receives a support score based on semantic alignment with retrieved evidence.

Experimental Setup:
We evaluate on a curated subset of arXiv scientific papers spanning natural language processing, computer vision, and machine learning systems. The average document length in the subset is 7,400 words, with many papers exceeding the effective context budget of single-pass prompting strategies. The base model is a 3B-parameter instruction-tuned language model adapted with QLoRA. We compare four systems: an extractive TextRank baseline, a zero-shot single-pass baseline, a prompt-engineered single-pass baseline, and our hierarchical section-aware model with evidence support scoring.

Results:
The proposed hierarchical model outperforms all baselines on long-document summarization. Compared with the prompt-engineered single-pass baseline, our model improves ROUGE-1 from 39.8 to 42.1, ROUGE-2 from 14.6 to 16.1, and ROUGE-L from 34.9 to 37.3. BERTScore also improves from 0.871 to 0.887. Gains are larger on papers above 6,000 words, where the hierarchical pipeline reduces failures caused by truncation and section imbalance.

Manual evaluation shows that the hierarchical system receives higher average scores on faithfulness and structure quality than the single-pass baseline. In particular, annotators note that the model more consistently mentions limitations and avoids overstating contributions. The evidence support module further improves usability by exposing which claims are weakly grounded. Across the manual subset, the rate of unsupported or weakly supported claims falls by 18 percent relative to the prompt-engineered baseline.

Discussion:
The results suggest that better long-document summarization does not come only from a larger context window. Instead, explicit control over document structure provides a strong inductive bias for scientific summarization. The model benefits from knowing whether it is reading an introduction, a method section, or a results section. This improves both content selection and final synthesis. The evidence support module does not prove factual correctness, but it provides a useful estimate of grounding and gives users a practical way to inspect the provenance of generated claims.

Limitations:
The system still has several limitations. First, the claim-level evidence score is retrieval-based and therefore may overestimate support when semantically similar but not fully sufficient evidence is retrieved. Second, the method may miss fine-grained mathematical derivations or nuanced assumptions described across multiple distant passages. Third, the current evaluation uses a curated arXiv subset and therefore does not fully measure cross-domain transfer to biomedical or legal scientific writing. Finally, running hierarchical summarization plus retrieval introduces more latency than a single-pass baseline.

Conclusion:
We presented a faithful long-document scientific summarization system that combines section-aware hierarchical inference with claim-level evidence retrieval. The method improves summary quality over strong single-pass baselines and produces outputs that are easier to inspect and trust.
